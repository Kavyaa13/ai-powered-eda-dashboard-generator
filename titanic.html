Executive Summary & Recommendations
Derived from the 418‑row test subset and canonical Titanic survival statistics.
Insights
1. Structural inequality by class
Survival probability declines sharply from 1st to 3rd class. Combining class with sex and age explains most of the outcome variance.
Model feature: Pclass Use as ordinal, not one‑hot only.
2. Data quality hotspots
Age is missing for 20.6% of passengers and cabin for 78.2%. Blind imputation risks introducing bias, especially for 3rd class passengers.
Treat “missing” as signal Encode missingness flags explicitly.
3. Family size sweet‑spot
Very large families (>4) show markedly lower survival. Solo and small‑family travellers have better outcomes.
Engineer "FamilySize" Bucket into 1, 2–4, 5+ for modelling.
Priority actions for modelling Roadmap next 3 steps
  1. Feature engineering: build composite variables (FamilySize, IsAlone, Title from name, Deck from cabin, FarePerPerson), as they capture non‑linear interactions found in previous Titanic models.
  2. Stratified evaluation: track model performance separately for sex×class segments (e.g. 3rd‑class males) to avoid hiding failure modes behind a good global accuracy.
  3. Scenario simulation: use the current dashboard priors to stress‑test policies such as “priority by class” vs “priority by family size” and quantify trade‑offs in survival counts.
